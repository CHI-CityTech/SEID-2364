## Overview

This assignment builds directly on your work in Assignment 4 and the in-class dissection exercises.

In Assignment 4, you evaluated a decision within a selected use case using ethical frameworks. This required you to interpret and justify an outcome, often treating the decision as if it were clearly defined and located.

In the Week 5 dissections, you began to unpack that assumption by restating your use case as a precise decision and identifying additional elements that influence the outcome.

In this assignment, you will shift from **evaluation** to **structure**.

Rather than asking whether a decision is ethical, you will analyze how that decision **emerges within a system**. Your goal is to describe the components, relationships, and conditions that make this type of decision possible.

This includes:
- identifying actors, targets, and contributing elements  
- examining how information is used and where it comes from  
- identifying how authority, ownership, responsibility, and harm are distributed  

You will also examine how decisions are not always located in a single place, but may be **distributed across processes, institutions, and technical systems**.

Your description must apply to **multiple decisions of this type**, not just a single instance. You will include at least one example showing how different inputs, conditions, or targets produce different outcomes within the same system.

This assignment prepares the transition from applying ethical frameworks to **describing and analyzing the systems in which those ethical decisions occur**.

## Part 2 — System Structure (Descriptive Layer)

### Task

Rewrite your use case from Assignment 4 as a **structured system description**.

Begin with a **single decision statement** that clearly identifies:
- who is making (or appears to make) the decision
- what the decision is
- who or what is affected (the target)

Example:
> "A bank denies a loan to an applicant"

This statement represents one instance of a broader system. Your task is to describe that system such that **other decisions of the same type could be understood using your description**.

You are not explaining whether the decision is ethical. You are identifying what must exist, structurally, for this type of decision to occur.

---

### System Components

Expand your decision into the following components:

- **Actor(s)** — entities that participate in or influence the system (individuals, organizations, or technical systems)
- **Target(s)** — who or what the decision acts upon
- **Determinants** — factors that shape or constrain the outcome (policies, rules, models, criteria)
- **Contributors** — actors or systems that influence the outcome without being the final decision-maker
- **Information / Data Inputs** — what information is available to actors making or influencing the decision

For **Information / Data Inputs**, you must also identify:
- **Source** — where the information originates (e.g., user input, dataset, institution)
- **Authority** — who governs, validates, or controls that information
- **Trust Basis** — why the information is accepted as valid or usable

---

### Guidelines

- Be specific (avoid vague terms like "the system" without definition)
- Use consistent terminology
- Do not assume a single decision-maker unless clearly justified
- If something influences the outcome, include it—even if indirect
- Your description should apply to decisions of this type, not just a single instance

Your goal is to move from a **single decision statement** to a **structured description of a decision-producing system**.

### Deliverable (system-components.md)

- Decision statement (1 sentence)
- Structured list of components
- Brief description of each component
- One contrasting example showing how different inputs, conditions, or targets produce a different outcome within the same system

## Part 3 — Interpretive Layer (Ethical Overlay) — Interpretive Layer (Ethical Overlay) — Interpretive Layer (Ethical Overlay)

### Task

Using your system description, identify how key ethical dimensions are distributed across the system.

You are not evaluating the decision using a framework. Instead, you are identifying where different forms of influence and accountability exist.

Focus on the distinction between:

- **Authority** — who has the power to make or enforce decisions
- **Ownership** — who controls data, systems, or processes
- **Responsibility** — who is accountable for outcomes (even if they did not directly decide)
- **Agency** — who or what is capable of acting within the system
- **Harm** — where negative impact occurs, and to whom

These may not align. In many systems:
- Authority and responsibility are separated
- Multiple actors contribute without having formal authority
- Harm may occur far from where decisions appear to be made

Your task is to identify and describe these relationships clearly.

### Deliverable (system-interpretation.md)

Short written response (1–2 paragraphs or structured bullets)
