## Part 1. System Selection and Harm Recap

I selected an AI hiring and applicant screening system that employers use to sort large numbers of applicants, which includes resume parsing, ranking models, automated assessments, chat-based screening, and video interview analysis. Its main actions are collecting applicant data, turning that data into features, scoring or ranking candidates, and deciding who moves forward or gets rejected.

This is a strong case study because the harms are complex. The system can improve speed and consistency. It can also exclude people in ways that are hard to see or challenge. The main harms are exclusion, especially for disabled applicants and applicants with unusual job histories, privacy risks from collecting sensitive data, and a lack of clarity about why someone was rejected. That lack of clarity also creates frustration.

The system places unequal burdens on different groups. A tool can look neutral and still disadvantage people who speak differently, need accommodations, have caregiving gaps, or do not perform well in standardized digital assessments. That makes AI hiring a clear example of a system with many forms of harm.

## Part 2. Core Framework From Class, Consequentialism

Consequentialism says the moral value of an action depends on its consequences. In utilitarian form, it asks whether an action creates the greatest overall good and the least overall harm. Walter Sinnott-Armstrong describes consequentialism as the view that normative properties depend only on consequences. Classical utilitarianism judges acts by how much they improve the world overall.

This framework evaluates harm by looking at negative effects on welfare, well-being, and overall outcomes. In AI hiring, a consequentialist asks which system design creates the best overall results for everyone affected. That includes employer benefits such as efficiency, lower hiring costs, and more consistent screening, with it also including harms to applicants, harms to public trust, and wider social costs if the system spreads exclusion at scale. Consequentialism is useful here because it forces decision-makers to compare real tradeoffs.

In AI hiring, consequentialism would support automation only if strong evidence shows that the system improves overall outcomes compared with realistic alternatives such as human review or hybrid review. If audited tools reduce favoritism, shorten hiring time, and widen access without causing major unfair burdens, a consequentialist could support them. The same framework could reject the system if the gains in efficiency are smaller than the harms from exclusion, discrimination, privacy loss, or error. Its main weakness is clear. Serious harms to a minority can disappear inside an average if the total welfare score still looks positive.

## Part 3. Independent Framework Research and Evaluation

## 3A. Description, The Capability Approach

For the independent framework, I selected the Capability Approach. The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy explains that this approach focuses on what people are actually able to do and be, instead of only the resources they have or the satisfaction they report.

This framework evaluates harm by asking whether a system narrows people’s real opportunities. In AI hiring, that means looking past efficiency. It asks whether the tool reduces applicants to narrow proxies, whether some groups face barriers that make the same formal process unequal in practice, and whether the system takes away agency, explanation, or fair access. The capability approach focuses on human flourishing, substantive equality, and the conditions people need to make meaningful choices.

## 3B. Framework Evaluation

The capability approach defines harm clearly when the problem is unequal access to opportunity. It works well when the same rule affects different groups in different ways. Two candidates can face the same digital screening process and still face very different burdens. One candidate may be at a major disadvantage because of disability, weak broadband access, accent bias, or a work history shaped by unpaid caregiving. The capability approach captures this difference because it asks what people can actually do with a process, not only what formal option they receive.


The framework also has limits, which can show what is ethically missing. It does not always show exactly how much efficiency loss, cost, or complexity an institution should accept to protect capabilities. Consider an employer that uses asynchronous video interviews scored by an AI model and offers a text-based alternative only if applicants disclose a disability first. The capability approach would criticize that design because some applicants face unequal burdens before they can even ask for help. The framework is less precise about how to balance the employer’s interest in speed and scale against the applicant’s interest in equal access.

That is the point where the framework gives an incomplete result. It gives a strong moral warning. It gives less procedural guidance when several imperfect redesigns are possible. An employer might ban automated video scoring, keep it as one weak signal, or create alternative pathways for every candidate. The framework identifies the core moral issue. It does not fully rank the practical options.

## Part 4. Application, Multi-Framework Harm Evaluation

Consider a company that uses an AI hiring pipeline with resume ranking, online testing, and automated video interview scoring. Internal review shows two results. The tool reduces recruiter workload and speeds up hiring. It also screens out some applicants at higher rates, especially disabled candidates and candidates with nonstandard communication styles, or pushes them into harder application paths. The company must decide whether to keep the system, redesign it, or remove major features such as automated video analysis.

Consequentialist Application

A consequentialist would focus on the harms and benefits with the largest expected effect on total welfare. That includes applicant exclusion, employer efficiency, hiring quality, legal risk, public trust, and broader labor market effects. This framework resolves competing harms by comparing their total size. If removing automated scoring reduces discrimination and wrongful rejection more than it increases cost and delay, then removal is justified. If hybrid review creates the best overall balance, then hybrid review is the best option.

## Capability Approach Application

The capability approach would focus on a different set of harms. Its main concern is whether applicants keep a genuine opportunity to compete for work on fair terms. In this case, the key harms are inaccurate rejection, statistical bias, reduced agency, weak explanation, and limited ability to challenge decisions. The framework does not treat all stakeholders as one group. It looks at how different groups experience the same process in different ways.

This approach resolves competing harms by asking which design best protects essential capabilities across affected groups. A process can be efficient and still be ethically flawed if it blocks disabled applicants from fair access. EEOC guidance warns that AI tools in employment can create discriminatory barriers and disability-related problems. The agency has stressed that these tools can become a pathway to discrimination if employers do not govern them carefully.

A capability-based response would likely remove or sharply limit automated video scoring, offer multiple assessment pathways, build accommodation and appeal into the default process, and require human review for major decisions. Compared with consequentialism, this framework is less willing to accept serious access barriers in exchange for efficiency gains.

## Part 5. Comparative Analysis

The two frameworks can reach the same result. In this case, both can support reducing or removing high-risk AI hiring features if the system causes exclusion, error, or discriminatory barriers. Both also recognize the need for governance. NIST, OECD, UNESCO, and U.S. enforcement agencies all stress trustworthiness, fairness, human rights, and oversight in AI systems.

The frameworks differ in what they treat as decisive. Consequentialism asks whether the full balance of outcomes is positive. It accepts tradeoffs and works best when reliable evidence exists about aggregate effects. The capability approach asks whether people keep genuine, dignified, and accessible opportunity. It works best when harms fall unevenly across groups or stay hidden inside averages.

Consequentialism also highlights a practical issue that the capability approach sometimes leaves open. Institutions often must choose among imperfect options under real constraints. In those cases, some balancing is unavoidable. Capability analysis shows what matters. It can be less precise about how to rank competing institutional costs and benefits.

I think the capability approach is the stronger moral lens for finding hidden injustice in AI hiring. Consequentialism is the stronger tool for comparing policy options after basic protections are in place. Neither framework is enough on its own.

## Part 6. Meta-Evaluation of Frameworks

Consequentialism works well when the task is to compare policies across a large system with many moving parts. It fits cases where decision-makers need to ask which design will reduce total harm the most. That matters in AI governance because large systems create effects across employers, workers, and institutions. This framework becomes weaker when key harms are hard to measure, when powerful actors control the metrics, or when severe burdens on a minority look acceptable because the aggregate outcome looks positive. Those weaknesses matter in modern technology.

I do not think either framework should stand alone in modern technological settings. Consequentialism needs side constraints so that access, dignity, and non-discrimination do not get traded away for aggregate gain. The capability approach needs practical support so that its moral insights can guide real design choices under real constraints. The strongest model would combine both. Capability-based protections can define what a system must not undermine. Consequentialist comparison can help choose among options that meet those protections.

## Part 7. Reflection

Ethical frameworks matter because identifying harm is not enough. In complex systems, people disagree about what counts as harm, whose harms matter most, and what to do when one remedy creates another problem. A framework gives structure to those disagreements. It makes the reasoning visible and open to challenge.

No single framework can capture harm completely. Harm can be material, psychological, social, political, institutional, and historical at the same time. Some frameworks measure tradeoffs well. Others are better at seeing exclusion, dignity, or structural inequality. A single framework will always leave something out.
