# Journal Entry Week 3 – Harm Analysis
## Part 1 – Speculative Harm Analysis
    System or decision context
The system which I'll be analyzing has to do with the hiring process used by a large company to filter and rank job applicants. In this kind of system, candidates submit resumes, application forms, and sometimes video interviews or online assessments. The AI agent then I’d say helps the employer decide which applicant moves forward, which should be reviewed more closely, and what is rejected. Even if a human manager is still involved at the end, the earlier stages of the process can strongly shape who gets seen at all. That means the system is not just administrative. It also affects how fairly candidates feel they are being treated. A hiring system looks efficient on the surface, but it can quietly shape opportunity long before anyone notices.
Some actions which would be performed in the system would be:
-   The system analyzes resumes and ranks candidates based on keywords, experience, education, or inferred fit.
-   It also scores assessments or video interviews and generates recommendations for recruiters.
-	It also filters out applicants before a human reviewer sees them.
3. Possible harms that could arise from those actions
Possible harms include unfair rejection of qualified candidates, loss of income or opportunity, and biased treatment of certain groups. Someone would be rejected and they wouldn’t know why, which could cause frustration. It can also damage trust in the employer, especially if the process seems inefficient. At a larger scale, if the AI has bias it can seriously cause problems in hiring.


## Part 2 – Core Readings
## Reading 1 – The Harm Principle
Mill’s harm principle pretty much tells me that its more important to look if an action causes harm to someone rather than if it is disliked. In the SEP entry, Mill’s view is summarized as the idea that power may be exercised over someone against their will only to prevent harm to others. In my case, for hiring, this pushes me to ask not only whether the company benefits from efficiency, but whether the system doesn’t make mistakes which could unintentionally harm someone.
## Reading 2 – Moral Responsibility / Moral Theory
The SEP entry on moral theory helped me see that ethical reasoning needs more than intuition. It explains that moral theories provide criteria for judging actions and can also provide decision procedures for guiding action. That matters here because hiring systems generate recurring decisions, and recurring decisions need a structure for judging tradeoffs, not just after-the-fact excuses. In other words, if a company regularly uses a system that can harm applicants, it needs an explicit way to decide what counts as acceptable risk, fairness, and accountability. 
## Reading 3 – Consequentialism
The SEP entry on consequentialism reframed the problem by asking what consequences matter and for whom. It describes consequentialism as the view that moral rightness depends on consequences, while also showing that consequentialist theories differ over which consequences count and how they should be assessed. For a hiring system, this matters because one cannot simply say “the model is useful” or “the company hires faster.” A serious analysis has to ask whether the total consequences include lost opportunities, exclusion, unequal treatment, and damaged trust, not just efficiency gains. 
## Revised Harm Analysis
After doing the readings, I would revise my analysis by saying that the harms in an AI hiring system are not only individual ones. They are also long-term. It lists core harms such as physical, psychological, economic, reputational, social, and institutional harm, and extended harms such as distributional harm, opportunity foreclosure, and accountability harm. 

## Types of harm
    The clearest harm is economic harm, because rejected applicants may lose access to wages and future career opportunities. There is also reputational harm if a candidate is inaccurately scored as unqualified or risky for the job. The AI can produce psychological harm through stress, humiliation, or frustration. At a broader level, it can create institutional harm when hiring procedures become less transparent and less contestable. The extended categories are especially useful here: opaque scoring can create epistemic harm, unequal false negatives can create distributional harm, and repeated automated filtering can create opportunity foreclosure and accountability harm when nobody can clearly explain or correct a decision. 
## Entities affected
    The most obvious affected entities are individual applicants, especially those who are screened out unfairly. Organizations can also be harmed by overreliance on flawed models, since they may lose qualified candidates and weaken trust in their hiring process. At the widest level, society is affected when access to work is distributed through opaque systems that can amplify existing inequality. NIST’s AI RMF emphasizes that AI risks emerge from the interaction between technical systems and their social context, and that without proper controls AI can amplify inequitable outcomes for individuals and communities. 
## Scale of harm
    The scale of harm can begin as local, when one applicant is rejected unfairly. It becomes institutional when the same flawed logic is used across a company’s entire recruitment scheme. It becomes societal when many employers use similar tools and the same groups repeatedly face disadvantage across sectors. This is why harm in sociotechnical systems should not be treated as a single event. It accumulates through repetition, normalization, and lack of meaningful appeal. 

## Part 4 – Reflection
The readings changed my understanding of harm by making it more structured and less superficial. Before, I mostly thought of harm as a direct bad outcome, such as someone unfairly losing a job, or someone injuring another. After reading all the sources, I see that harm  also involves the decision process itself, the distribution of risk, and the long-term social effects of repeated system use. Mill’s harm principle made me focus on harms to others rather than mere inconvenience. The moral theory reading showed why societies need usable decision procedures when repeated decisions affect people’s lives. Consequentialism added another layer by showing that we often have to compare multiple harms and benefits at once, even when they are hard to measure on the same scale. In this hiring case, frameworks are necessary because people will disagree about what matters most: efficiency, fairness, equality of opportunity, transparency, or outcomes. Without a framework, decisions become biased based on who they benefit most. With a framework, disagreement does not disappear, it is much more clear on a decision.
