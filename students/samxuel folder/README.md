
1. When no option avoids harm, what does it mean to choose the right action?
Choosing the right action means selecting the option that can be most reasonably justified within a consistent ethical framework, while acknowledging that harm is unavoidable. The focus shifts from eliminating harm to minimizing or justifying it.

2. Example of two people reaching different but defensible conclusions:
In a scenario where one must choose between saving five people or one person, a utilitarian may choose to save the five to minimize total harm, while a deontologist may refuse to actively sacrifice the one person, prioritizing moral rules over outcomes. Both positions are logically consistent within their frameworks.

3. Different ways people decide between competing harms:
People may minimize total harm, follow moral rules, prioritize fairness, protect vulnerable individuals, consider responsibility, or rely on intuition. In practice, most decisions involve a combination of these approaches rather than a single method.
